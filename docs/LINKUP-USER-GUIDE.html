Your LinkUp guide

Everything here is written for real people—not engineers. Short sentences. No jargon. You’ve got this.

What is LinkUp?

LinkUp helps you discover meetups near you, chat with the person hosting, agree on the details, and—when money is involved—pay in a safer, structured way.

Think of it like this:

  • You browse plans people have posted (coffee, fitness, events, paid experiences—whatever fits the community).
  • You send an offer or message to join the conversation.
  • You agree on time, place, and price when it’s a fit.
  • For paid meetups, money can sit in a holding step until things go as agreed—so both sides have more peace of mind.

LinkUp is built for real-world meetups and trust. That’s why some steps ask you to verify who you are before you host, negotiate, or pay through the app.

How the app is laid out

After you’re set up, you’ll mostly live in three tabs:

TabWhat it’s for
PlansYour home base—nearby plans, create your own, open something interesting.
MessagesChats with people you’re talking to.
ProfileYou—photos, bio, settings, verification, account stuff.

When you first land in the app, you’ll usually open on Plans. That’s on purpose: it’s the main “what’s happening around me?” screen.

Getting started (first time)

1. Create an account

Sign up with email, phone, or a sign-in option the app offers (like Google). If the app asks you to confirm your email or a code, that’s normal—it’s making sure it’s really you.

2. Build your profile (onboarding)

You’ll walk through a few friendly steps—things like:

  • Photos and name
  • A short bio and interests
  • Who you’d like to meet and how far you’re willing to browse
  • A quick safety moment (tips, optional choices)

Important: Finishing your profile is not the same as identity verification. You can enter the app and look around after onboarding. Verification comes in when you want to do trust-heavy things (see below).

3. Location (optional but helpful)

If the app asks for location, it’s usually to show nearby plans and distances. You can say no—but near you works better with it.

Browsing plans (everyone can do this)

On the Plans tab you’ll see a feed of nearby plans.

  • Tap a plan to see details—time ideas, price (if any), host info, and how to engage.
  • Use filters or chips if your app shows them (distance, categories, etc.).

You do not need to be verified to scroll and explore. LinkUp wants you to see what’s out there first.

Identity verification—why it exists

Some actions are sensitive: posting a plan, sending offers, and moving money in the app. To reduce scams and keep meetups safer, LinkUp asks you to verify your identity before those actions.

You might see:

  • A gentle “Unlock more” prompt (you can skip and come back).
  • A clearer “Verification required” screen if you tap something that needs it—like Create or Send offer.

Verification is about trust—not about showing your ID on your public profile. Your documents are used to confirm you’re real, not to decorate your card.

What the verification flow feels like

When you start, you’ll move through simple steps—something like:

  1. Trust intro — why LinkUp does this and what it unlocks.
  2. Choose your ID type — e.g. national ID, passport, driver’s license, or voter card.
  3. Photo of your ID — clear, no glare; you can often retake or upload from your gallery.
  4. Short selfie video — quick liveness check (usually a few seconds).
  5. Consent — you agree to how verification data is used.
  6. Review — your submission is in progress; you’ll get an outcome (approved or needs another try).

You can often save progress and come back. If you’re already waiting on a decision, the app should tell you—you’re not “starting over” from zero; you can resume or check status.

Creating a plan (verified hosts)

Want to host? Tap create (often a + on the Plans screen).

You’ll fill in what people need to know—title, description, time window, location area, price if it’s paid, and who can see it (for example public, nearby only, or friends-style visibility depending on the app).

If you’re not verified yet, the app will explain and offer Start verification (or Resume if you’re already in the flow). That’s normal—not a bug.

Offers and negotiation (verified)

When you’re interested in someone’s plan, you’ll send an offer or open the negotiation experience the app provides.

  • You might propose a price or terms, counter, or accept—like hailing a ride or agreeing on a gig, but for meetups.
  • Chat often ties in here: you and the other person can talk in Messages so details stay clear.

Sending offers is gated for verified users—same trust idea as hosting.

Agreeing on the meetup

When you and the host (or guest) are aligned, the host may accept an offer. Then you’ll usually see an agreement or summary screen:

  • When and where (or what you agreed)
  • Price (if there is one)
  • Next step—either “you’re good to go” for a free hangout, or continue to payment if money is involved

Read it like you would a confirmation email before a trip. If something’s wrong, don’t confirm—message the other person or step back until it’s clear.

How payments work (the simple version)

For paid plans, LinkUp uses a structured payment path so money isn’t just sent into the void.

In plain words:

  • The payer completes payment through a secure checkout (the app works with a trusted payment partner).
  • Funds are held according to the plan’s escrow flow—meaning they’re set aside until the meetup side of the agreement is honored or a support path is used.
  • When things go as agreed, funds can move to the host (release rules depend on product design—who taps “complete,” timers, etc.).
  • If something goes wrong, there should be a path to dispute or get help so a human can review.

You generally need to be verified to fund or complete escrow steps. That protects everyone.

Messages

Open the Messages tab to see conversations.

  • Threads are between you and one other person (in the current app design).
  • You might open a chat from a plan or from the inbox—same conversation.

Messaging is usually available before you’re verified, but there may be limits for brand-new or unverified accounts (to slow down spam). Hosting, offering, and paying are stricter—that’s by design.

Your profile and account

In Profile you can:

  • Edit photos, bio, preferences—keep it fresh.
  • See if you’re Verified or Unverified (and tap through to verification if you want).
  • Open settings—privacy, notifications, support, logout, and similar.

Premium (if offered) is about extra perks—like more visibility or filters—not a substitute for verification. You can’t pay to skip identity checks for hosting, offers, or escrow.

Staying safe (the human stuff)

  • Meet in public first when you don’t know someone well.
  • Share plans with a friend: who, where, when.
  • Keep chat in the app when you can—helps if you ever need support.
  • If someone pressures you to pay outside the app or skip steps, that’s a red flag.
  • Use report or block tools if the app offers them—and Support for anything serious.

LinkUp can’t promise every person is perfect—but verification, structured payments, and clear agreements exist to stack the odds in favor of safer, clearer meetups.

Help when something breaks

If the app errors, payment confuses you, or you feel unsafe:

  • Open Support from settings or profile (wording may vary slightly).
  • You can often submit a ticket or follow prompts for disputes tied to a payment.

You’re not bothering anyone—that’s what Support is for.
